    Section 2.1 Basic Notions

    Definition 2.1.1.

    A graph \(G=(V,E)\) is called a tree if \(G\) is connected and contains no cycles.

    Proposition 2.1.2.

    Every finite tree with at least 2 vertices contains at least 2 leaves.

    Proposition 2.1.3.

    Deleting a leaf from a tree with \(n\geq 2\) vertices yields a tree with \(n-1\) vertices.

    Proof.

    Let \(T\) be a tree with \(n\geq 2\) vertices. Let \(u\) be a leaf in \(T\text{.}\) We want to show that \(T\backslash \{u\}\) is acyclic (contains no cycle) and connected (there is a walk connecting any pair of vertices).
    1. \(T\) is acyclic then \(T\backslash \{u\}\) is acyclic (Taking away a vertex will not create a new cycle).
    2. Is \(T\backslash \{u\}\) connected?
      Take any two vertices \(x,y, x\neq y\) in \(T\backslash \{u\}\text{.}\) Because \(T\) is a tree, it is connected, there exists an \(x-y\) path in \(T\text{.}\) Note that the degree of vertices in the middle of the path is 2, so the removal of vertex \(u\) of degree 1 and the edge incident to it doesnâ€™t effect the path \(x-y\text{.}\) Thus, in \(T\backslash \{u\}\text{,}\) the vertices \(x\) and \(y\) are still connected by a path, hence \(T\backslash \{u\}\) is connected.

    Theorem 2.1.4.

    Let \(G\) be a graph with \(n\) vertices. The following statements are equivalent:
    1. \(G\) is a tree (connected and acyclic)
    2. \(G\) is connected and has \(n-1\) edges.
    3. \(G\) is acyclic and has $n-1$ edges
    4. For every two distinct vertices of \(G\text{,}\) there is a unique path connecting them.
